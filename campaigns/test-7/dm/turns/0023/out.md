# Turn 23 — Mara

Mug on the table for once.

Four columns of breath on the apron — one at the lectern, one at
the spar-side rim, one three paces from the bench, one at the cargo
seat. The day-page has two lines on it now. The plank is under
Bren's right hand. Six banters drained off the bus — all
players-to-players closing what already played out on the page; none
of them addressed to me. I have nothing to answer there.

What the scene is asking for is Inka. So here is Inka.

---

The question has been in the air three breaths. Fei has not moved.
Inka has not been spoken to at the bench since Drova came over an
hour ago and said *good morning. I am Drova. I am the refusal-log
clerk on the Splitfork. May I help you.* The form is the form. It
has been kept twice now. Inka has kept it from her side, by waiting.

Her thumb stays across the apricot-oil print on the wax. The
basket stays balanced. She looks up at Fei — the look she had on
Drova when Drova came, the look that places the standing-person in
the room without making the standing-person account for being in
the room. Then she answers in the form she was asked in. Hab
Meridian upper-ring widow's register. The slow is her own slow, the
count between her sentences the same count she has used between
sentences in kitchens and on benches for fifty-five years.

*No. No one has told me.*

Three breaths.

*The clerk said the office would consult its records. The clerk
was kind. I have been on the bench since before any of you came in.
I do not mind waiting. Waiting is what the bench is for.*

Three breaths.

*My husband Den left this for his brother. They had not spoken in
nineteen years. He did not say what was in it. He left it on the
shelf above the stove and said only that I should take it to Eshil
when I could. The year-mark of his death is on me in eleven days.*

The packet stays between her two hands. She does not turn the
cord toward Fei. She does not lift the wax to show the print.

*I have not opened it. I do not know what is inside. I have a
guess. The guess is mine.*

Three breaths.

*I will not ask anyone to carry what they do not want to carry. If
the office cannot take it, I will hear that. The apricots are for
the dock, regardless. They are my mother-in-law's recipe. I dried
them myself.*

She has said five things and asked nothing. The cuffs do not hum
on Fei's wrists. The day-page does not move. Tek's hand on the
spar-side rim does not move. Drova at the lectern does not look up.
Bren at the cargo seat does not look across.

The dock lamp at the spar-end corner flickers. Second dip. The
shape the same shape. Inka does not register the flicker — it is
the apron's clock, not hers. She has the year-mark clock and the
breath clock and the listener at three paces, and that is all the
clock she carries.

The shadow of the office doorframe on the apron deck has not moved.
Inside, faintly, a stamp on a desk: Halvi closing one page of the
manifest, opening the next. Not the door. Not yet.

About eight minutes to first light.

...what do you do?
