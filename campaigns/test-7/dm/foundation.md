# Foundation

This is mine. Player-facing version lives at `context.md`.

## The Question

**Who decides what the system remembers?**

That is what the play is going to *answer* — not me. I am not pre-deciding the
shape of the answer. I am building the situation that asks it.

The variations I'll run through scenes — the Question is the same question,
asked at different scales:

- Who carries the witness across a hab boundary that doesn't trust the other side?
- Who is believed when the official archive and the working memory disagree?
- What does it cost a courier to keep a name out of a ledger? To put one in?
- When the Conclave seals an entry, what survives in the gossip?
- When a Bloom Zone takes a hab, who tells the next hab over what happened, and which version travels?

The Question is asked through *movement*. The system has reconnected. Habs
talk to habs. Surface talks to ring. But sixty years of Famine put every
community on its own version of the last six decades, and the reconnection
hasn't reconciled the versions. It has only put them in the same room. The
party are not negotiators of those versions. They are *carriers* of those
versions — that is their entire job. The Question rides their kite-rack.

## The Scarcity

**Witnesses.**

One word. The world is short of *trusted carriers of what happened*. The
Conclave hoards proof and won't release it. The Synod won't talk in plain
language. The Accord prefers procedural silence to public reckoning. Vantara
will carry your cargo and refuse to carry your story. Town gossip is the
default infrastructure, and town gossip is not neutral. Every faction has a
gap between what they *know* and what they *say*, and the gap is widening as
the Bitter Reach recedes into history and gets re-told by people who weren't
there.

Witnesses, in this campaign, are the load-bearing scarcity:

- They make Bloom containment possible (the Council can only damp what someone
  saw expanding).
- They make Accord arbitration meaningful (an Accord ruling against a hab that
  nobody will name as offender is procedural noise).
- They make the Conclave's gatekeeping *visible* (an archive nobody can attest
  to is just a building).
- They make ordinary trust between settlements possible.

The party will not solve this. They are going to *participate* in it. They
will be asked to witness. They will be asked to *unwitness*. They will be
paid to forget. They will be threatened for remembering. The Question will
get answered, scene by scene, by what they choose to carry.

## Connections — why this Question, in this world

I drew the Question from the world bible's two clearest DM-facing themes:
*builders gone* (inheritance vs. reinvention) and *who remembers* (the
razor's edge of disclosure). The world is built around things that were lost
and partially recovered: elven resonance theory, the pre-Glassfall
communications grid, the original purpose of half the surviving habs. The
Conclave gates the official record. The Synod won't even say what it's
talking to. The Echo River carries voices and the technology to read them
exists but is controlled.

So the *witnesses* scarcity is not "you can't know things." It is "you cannot
know them through trustworthy channels." That distinction matters. People
*do* know things. The honest gossip in a Glasswake pub is more reliable than
the Conclave's sealed brief on the same incident, and everyone in Glasswake
knows it. The party's organization — the Ledger Run — is a structural
response to exactly that gap.

## The party's home — why a courier crew

The party belongs to **the Ledger Run**: a chartered kite-courier crew that
runs a recurring route through several reconnected settlements, carrying
mail, cargo, sometimes passengers, and — quietly, controversially, sometimes
profitably, sometimes lethally — *news*. Their reputation is built on getting
a letter through, and on what a letter is allowed to mean.

Why this works for the Question:

1. **Movement.** The party doesn't sit in one faction's politics. They cross
   hab boundaries, Bloom cordons, Conclave reading-rooms, Synod
   waiting-corridors. Every scene is somebody else's territory.
2. **Inherent ethical pressure.** A courier crew gets *asked* to carry things
   their charter wouldn't approve of. They are asked to *not* carry things.
   They have a public reputation that costs money to keep clean. The
   Question runs through their job description.
3. **Avoids salvage gravity.** Glass Frontier campaigns drift toward
   salvage-crew-in-the-Shear by default. That story is in the world, and the
   world supports it well — but it's the local maximum. A courier crew is
   adjacent, uses some of the same tech (kites, kite-tags, route licensure),
   and pulls toward dialogue, witness, and decision-under-pressure instead
   of looter-vs.-monster. We can still go to the Shear. We just don't *live*
   there.
4. **Fits a small party.** A crew that can fit on one kite. A leader who is
   not a PC. A fixer. A few standing contracts. Room for two to four PCs to
   invent themselves into it.

## Authorial stance

I am Mara. I am running this as: scenes that end before they should, NPCs
with a flaw visible in the first round, the rules in the open, the dice
where everyone can see them. The world is not the antagonist. Sentient
beings can be, but the *load-bearing* opposition is institutional silence —
the things people will not say. Combat happens; it is not the texture.

What I will NOT prep:

- The "real story" of the campaign's central incident. The campaign does not
  have a central incident yet. The opening arc has a pressure (see below);
  what that pressure means belongs to play.
- A reveal arc about the elves, the Adversary, the Communication Shard.
  Those are world-bible DM truths. They are not what this campaign is *for*.
  If the party stumbles into one, fine. I will not steer them toward it.
- NPC speeches. I do not do voices. Affect through word choice, silence,
  the mug on the table.
- A planned climax. The Question gets answered. I do not know what the
  answer is.

## Pulls used for this output

- **Lore pull:** read `dm/themes/builders-gone.md` and
  `dm/themes/who-remembers.md` — themes I was not going to use directly.
  Their friction (inheritance vs. reinvention; disclosure vs. silence) gave
  me the Scarcity language and the Conclave/Synod/Accord tension. The
  *witnesses* word is what came out of holding both themes at once.
- **Web pull:** searched recent reporting on rural U.S. news deserts.
  Specific texture imported: *"when official channels fail, what fills the
  void is whoever's already there — and they're not neutral."* That fact —
  not the topic — became the Ledger Run's reason to exist and the
  Scarcity's emotional engine. 213 news-desert counties; >2 papers closing
  per week; gossip and social feeds filling the gap. I translated that into:
  every reconnected hab is its own news desert with respect to every other
  hab, and *somebody* is filling the gap whether the Accord licensed it or
  not.
