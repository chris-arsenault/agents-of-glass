# The Accord Clerks

A campaign-specific faction *distinct from* the Tempered Accord institution. The Accord (`shared/lore/npcs/factions/tempered-accord.md`) is the mediation framework. The Accord *clerks* are the working bureaucracy that runs the framework — the people who actually file, schedule, draft, and decide at the procedural level. They have their own interests, their own internal politics, and their own clock. They are the party's actual interface with the Accord.

## One-sentence identity

The people who keep the Accord functional, who are paid badly to do it, and who quietly resent every faction that treats them as filing infrastructure.

## Why they're their own faction this campaign

Two reasons:

1. **Article 14 routes through them.** Vantara's amendment lives at the clerks' level for the entirety of its review cycle. The clerks decide the order things get heard, the scope of public comment, and the framing of the recommendation that goes to the Accord proper. The clerks' habits and resentments will shape the amendment's fate as much as Vantara's lobbying will.
2. **The refusal log lives in their cabinets.** The Ledger Run files a copy of the refusal log with the Accord clerks every quarter. Officially, the clerks file it without reading it. Unofficially, several clerks read every page, and one of them indexes it.

The Accord clerks are *not* the Accord. The Accord is what the clerks make happen. This distinction matters in this campaign because the Accord-as-institution moves slowly and predictably; the clerks move idiosyncratically and on grudges.

## Goal — right now

**Get through the quarterly filing crunch without taking blame for any decision.** The clerks' professional ethic is: clear the queue, attribute outcomes to the framework, never be the named author of a controversial recommendation. The current crunch — Article 14, three contested hab-boundary disputes, two refused-contact filings, an unprecedented number of refusal-log citations — is straining this ethic.

The clerks' shared internal wish: *somebody else, please, decide whose comment record carries weight.* They will, in practice, defer to whichever party gives them the most procedurally-clean grounds to defer.

## Tier

Regional, in their own self-conception. System-wide in actual reach. They do not see themselves as a faction. They are.

## Hold

- **The filing room** in the Accord complex at Sithari. Cabinets, indexes, the schedule. The standing-court drop where the Ledger Run leaves its quarterly log.
- **The order of the queue.** A filing's place in line is set by the clerk who logs it. There are accepted procedures. There is also discretion.
- **The framing of the recommendation.** Clerks draft the language that goes to the Accord proper. The framing shapes the vote.
- **Tal Vorenis** (see `dm/notes/npcs/tal-vorenis.md`), the mid-ranking clerk who actually reads the refusal log and indexes it. He is not in charge. He is one of about forty clerks at his level. He matters because of what he does on his own time.

## Clock — *the clerks defer*

3 segments. Slower clock than Vantara's, faster than the Conclave's, runs in parallel.

1. **The crunch worsens.** Filing volume exceeds the clerks' procedural capacity. Backlog grows.
2. **The clerks adopt a default.** Under load, contested filings get the default ruling — usually whichever ruling requires the least clerk-side authorship. For Article 14, this is *advance the amendment to the Accord with neutral framing,* which Vantara wants, because Vantara wrote the neutral framing.
3. **The default holds.** The amendment goes to the Accord with the framing Vantara prefers. Other contested filings get the same treatment. The clerks survive the crunch by becoming a passive surface.

The party can disrupt this by giving Tal Vorenis a reason to draft the framing himself instead of taking the default. He will not do this without a reason. He may do it with one.

## Relationships — this campaign

- **Vantara:** the lobbyists know the clerks by face. The clerks resent this and accept lunches. Both things are true.
- **Echo Ledger Conclave:** the clerks file Conclave seal recommendations as procedure. A few clerks have noticed Conclave dossiers showing up as Vantara filing footnotes. Tal Vorenis has noticed.
- **The Ledger Run:** the clerks file the refusal log. Tal reads it. The crew has been polite to him over twenty quarterly drop-offs and he has been polite back. That is the entire surface relationship. Underneath, he respects the work.
- **The Synod:** the clerks have a folder labeled *Synod correspondence*. It contains nothing. Nobody knows whether this is filing failure or accurate record-keeping.
- **Glasswake municipality:** routine. Standing contracts, no current friction.

## Internal split (DM-only)

The clerks are split between the **Defaulters** (the majority — clear the queue, attribute outcomes to the framework, take no blame) and the **Authors** (a quiet minority — draft the framing actively, take responsibility, accept the political cost). Tal is an Author. He is one of perhaps four. The Defaulters are not hostile to the Authors; they consider them naive. The Authors consider the Defaulters complicit. Neither group says any of this out loud.

If the players give an Author a reason to act, the action lands. If the players give the Defaulters a reason to act, nothing happens — the default is always available.

## What the clerks can be paid in

Not money. The clerks are paid by the Accord. What they can be paid in:

- **Procedural cover** — a paper trail that justifies an active framing, so the clerk who drafts it isn't named as the source.
- **A clean filing on a different contested matter** — if the crew can clear something off the clerks' queue, the clerk who benefits remembers.
- **Information that lets a clerk file a recommendation faster** — research the clerks don't have time to do themselves.
- **Public attribution to the framework, not the clerk** — if the players win a reframing, they cannot crow about it in a way that names the clerk who drafted it. This is non-negotiable.
