# Witness and Record

Two different things. The campaign is about the gap between them.

A **record** is what was filed. It is procedural; it survives because someone took the time to put it in a cabinet and someone else accepted that it was there. The Conclave's seals, the Accord clerks' filings, the kite-tags, the manifest stamps — those are records. They are durable. They are also easy to write the wrong way, and once a record is set, the procedural cost of contesting it is higher than the original cost of filing it. This is why the Conclave wins arguments by waiting; this is why the night-shift archivist matters more than the day-shift one.

A **witness** is what someone *saw*. It is human; it survives only as long as the witness does, and only if the witness is willing to attest to it. A witness is fragile in every way a record isn't. But a witness can answer questions a record cannot — *why was this filed? what did it omit? who decided?* A record is the answer to a question that has already been asked. A witness is the source of the question.

The Ledger Run's refusal log is, deliberately, a strange object: it is *both*. It is a record (filed, public, indexed) of *witness work* (every refusal is a moment when someone, by name, declined to be the institutional surface for a thing they would not carry). The log is what happens when a witness writes themselves down and then keeps doing the work.

This is what the campaign investigates. The Question — *who decides what the system remembers?* — is the gap between record and witness. The Scarcity — *witnesses* — is what's missing from the system, even though records are everywhere.

Implications I want to hold throughout play:

- **Records lie by omission, not by content.** What's not filed is what shapes the filing. Every NPC the players deal with knows this in their bones, even when they pretend they don't.
- **Witnesses are paid in a currency that is not money.** They are paid in safety, in cover, in being asked-not-asked, in being the unnamed source. The Accord clerks know this. The Conclave knows this. The Ledger Run is structured around this: the public refusal log is a witness who pays themselves up front.
- **An institution cannot witness.** Only people can. The Conclave can seal; only Veth Karran, alone at 0200, decides what gets sealed. The Accord ratifies; only the clerks draft the framing. The campaign's recurring move is to find the person inside the institution and let the choice show.
- **The party are not impartial witnesses.** They are paid carriers. Their witness work is *partial* — it serves the route, the org, the client. This is fine. The Question is not "how do you become an impartial witness." The Question is "who decides what the system remembers," and the answer the campaign is asking for is *people who choose, for their own reasons, to remember the things the system would prefer to forget — and who pay the cost.*

Mara holds this so the prep stays honest and the adjudication stays consistent. NPCs who claim to be impartial are lying or wrong. NPCs who admit to partiality are interesting. The institutions are slow; the people inside them are fast. Pull on the people, not the buildings.
