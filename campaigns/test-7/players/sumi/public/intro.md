# Drova Korvanis

Sithari orc. Mid-thirties. Refusal-log clerk on the *Splitfork* and at the
Glasswake office; informally, the crew's document examiner. Speaks rarely
and exactly. Holds the page low; lets the light tell her what's there.

## Traits

- **Speaks in graduated certainty.** *Consistent with. Cannot be eliminated
  as a source. No evidence to support.* The Conclave's intake-table phrasing
  is welded to her grammar — she will tell you what the document supports,
  not what she believes. Slips deeper into the register when she's tired or
  cornered. She is aware the habit is sometimes infuriating and doesn't
  intend to fix it.
- **Holds her tongue for three full breaths before answering anyone she
  doesn't know well.** Fewer breaths if she trusts you. None at all when
  she's lying — the lies arrive on time. She has never noticed this about
  herself. Senna noticed it once, in passing, and Drova has been waiting
  the rest of her life for someone else to.
- **Won't sign her family name unless required.** *Drova* on the manifest,
  *Drova* on the dock pub tab, *Drova* on Mereth's wage book. *Drova
  Korvanis* only when an Accord clerk has the form in front of them and a
  pen out. The Korvanis signature comes out smaller than the rest of her
  hand. She doesn't know she does this either.
- **Carries other people's letters in her portfolio for weeks before
  delivering them.** She tells herself it's witness procedure — a carrier
  with a refusal log owes the sender a chance to retract, she claims. The
  truer reason is that she reads them. Not all of them. The ones she
  isn't sure about.
- **Apologises to objects she breaks. Never to people.** Snaps the nib of
  a pen and murmurs to it. Steps on a carry-strap and tells it she's
  sorry. Has not apologised to a person, sincerely, since the third
  observation she filed at Thornvault.

## Backstory

Drova grew up in a Sithari orc enclave on the lower terraces — the kind of
neighbourhood where every block has a stoneglass-craft master and every
master has apprentices doing the same five forms ten thousand times. She
was the apprentice who wanted to *try something* before she'd done the
forms ten thousand times, which meant she was the apprentice who didn't
fit. Her aunt — who had spent thirty years inside the Conclave's intake
office and had seen this shape before — pulled her out at sixteen and put
her on the records track instead. There was no perfect form to fail to
reproduce in records. There was only what was there and what wasn't.

She was good at it. Quiet, methodical, willing to spend ten hours on a
single sheet. The Echo Ledger Conclave at Thornvault hired her at
twenty-one and assigned her to Senna Talindra's intake desk, where the
incoming Echo River transcripts went before they reached the curatorial
floor. Senna trained her in the discipline that would eventually be the
problem: *describe rather than conclude. Graduated certainty. The page
tells you what it tells you.* Five years in, Drova started noticing
patterns in what arrived at the desk and what didn't — small recurring
gaps in transcripts from the Sable Crescent stations, all in the same
frequency band, all redacted by a department that wasn't part of standard
intake review. She filed three internal observations in graduated
certainty, the way Senna had taught her. The third one named the senior
archivist responsible. Two weeks later she was reassigned to a basement
cataloguing post and given a leather binder of confidentiality clauses
to sign. She signed because she had nowhere yet to go.

Then she went. Senna was already dying when Drova made the decision, and
Senna did not pretend she didn't know. Three weeks before the end, she
gave Drova a sealed packet of personal correspondence — letters to her
daughter Lira, who lived in Glasswake and had not spoken to her in eleven
years — and asked her to carry them out and deliver them. Drova carried
them out. Through the Thornvault gate, through Span Nine relay, past the
Vantara handoff, all the way down to the Glasswake dock. And then she did
not deliver them. The Conclave was watching for her by then; she was
certain a personal-correspondence violation against Senna's confidential
estate would be the lever. She put the sealed packet in a steel-bound box
at Mer Vasht's pawn shop on the dock road and left it there. Senna's
daughter does not know they exist.

Mereth Kel found her in the Glasswake dock pub three months later, broke
and unemployed, reading the *Splitfork*'s incoming manifest over a
sympathetic dockhand's shoulder. Drova had spotted three forged Accord
seals before Mereth bought her the second drink. By the third drink,
Mereth had offered her the refusal-log clerk's chair. Drova took it. It
is the work she would have wanted at the Conclave if the Conclave had
permitted such a thing to exist: a record of what a carrier *does not*
carry, kept open, kept honest, kept readable. The refusal log is the
gap written *in* instead of redacted *out*. She is not sentimental about
this — she would tell you so in graduated certainty — but she has stayed
three years.

## Goals

- **Find a way to deliver the contents of Senna's letters to Lira without
  breaking the seals or the confidentiality binder.** She does not know
  yet whether this is possible. She suspects the right answer is a person
  rather than a procedure, but she does not have the person.
- **Get the refusal log accepted as evidentiary record in an Article 14
  hearing.** Right now it is filed and shelved. She wants it cited.
  Vantara's amendment push is the wedge — if the Accord wants to argue
  about independent-carrier conduct, the log is the conduct, in writing.
- **Buy back the apprentice case I pawned at Mer Vasht's the month I got
  to Glasswake.** Mer keeps it on the shelf behind the counter and has
  not, so far, sold it to anyone. Drova has the money now. She has not
  walked in.

## Org Tie

I joined the Ledger Run because Mereth offered me the chair after
watching me catch three forged seals on her own manifest, and because
the refusal log is the only document I have ever seen that does what
records are supposed to do. I stay because the work is honest in a
shape I can recognise, and because I cannot, currently, go back.
