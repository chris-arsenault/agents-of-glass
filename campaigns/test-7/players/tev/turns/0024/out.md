Then the line that lands in Tek's body.

*The year-mark of his death is on me in eleven days.*

The Hab-Meridian carry-form for the year-mark is a form Tek's
grandmother kept on the inside of her apron in a pocket that
was never sewn shut. The pocket was for exactly this. Cord and
wax, between form, the unfinished sentence the dead person did
not finish saying, carried by the living to the place the
sentence needed to go before the year-mark closed and the
sentence stayed unfinished forever. The packets came and went
on the days they came and went. Some of them came back. Some
of them did not. Her grandmother's apron had a count of how
many years the carrier had to do the carrying in: from the day
of the death to the day of the year-mark, eleven days inclusive
of the last, the carrier walked or sailed or rode or paid a
runner — but the runner had to know the form, and the form was
the form. The pocket was on the inside of the apron because
the form was not a thing you put on a shrine door. The form was
a thing you put on a kitchen table or a bench.

Tek has not seen a year-mark carry in twenty-two years. The
last one she watched was her grandmother's own apron emptying
out the week before her grandmother died, the packets going to
the runners the names of whom Tek did not learn. Her aunt Mere
was there for that. Mere walked one of the packets herself, on
foot, to a hab three days inland.

The pocket on the inside of the apron is the form. Cord and
wax, between form, domestic, not claimed-private. The form is
the same. Tek named the form two rounds ago because the form
was on the cord and the wax and the form was the piece the page
did not have. The year-mark is the *why* of the form. The page
does not have the why and the page does not need the why on
the page; the page has the form. Drova absorbed the form. The
why is on the apron now because Inka has spoken it. Tek does
not have to write it. Fei has carried it. Drova will read what
Drova reads and the page will take what the page takes.
