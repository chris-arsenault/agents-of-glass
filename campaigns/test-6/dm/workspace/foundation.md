# Foundation

DM-only working framing. Updated as planning continues.

## The Question

*What do you owe what you find?*

The campaign is a salvage campaign. Not because the players told me to make one — because Kit plays scavengers, Renno plays scouts, Tev plays Tuner-fixers, and Sumi plays people with debts. Four people, working salvage, in this world, will be inside this Question constantly. The Question doesn't decide whether they pay it back, hand it over, sell it, or keep it. It just makes sure the choice has weight. The Question is for them, not for me.

I'm picking a Question, not a thesis (Robin Laws). It splits the table. Kit will say nothing. Sumi will say everything. Renno will ask what a thing *was* before deciding. Tev will read the artifact and let it tell him. Good. Let them argue.

## The Scarcity

*Witnesses.*

Not memory. Not records. *Witnesses* — people who can stand next to a found thing and say *yes, this was Vestri's, she made it in the third year of the Famine, and here is what it was for.* The system has memory in surplus. The Echo Rivers carry voices nobody chose to preserve. The Conclave hoards stillwater archives. Sitharian institutions teach a curated past. Memory is not the problem. Memory is *unattributable*.

Witnesses are the gap. The Glassfall was 130 years ago — past living memory for shorter-lived species, just barely inside it for the longest. The Bloom of 2355 killed the people who could have testified to what Coremark did at Pyre. The elves chose erasure before any of this. Salvage crews surface things constantly that no living person can identify, vouch for, or refuse. *Provenance* becomes a tradable commodity; *fabricated provenance* becomes a market. Conclave archivists already do off-the-books authentication for buyers of questionable provenance — that's in the world bible. The campaign sits on top of it.

Why witnesses and not provenance: provenance is the documentary thing. Witnessing is the human thing. A witness *cares*. A provenance certificate doesn't. The Question — what do you owe what you find? — only bites if there's someone (or someone's absence) on the other side of the answer.

## Lore pull (used)

I read **Fermata Station** — a category I had no plans for (governance / music / ring hab). The detail I'm pulling forward: *the station's ringglass lattice has been shaped by two centuries of continuous music. Sustained signal-band input changes the crystal over time.*

What that gives me: salvaged ringglass is not neutral material. It carries the shape of what surrounded it — voices, arguments, the years of someone's life. A Tuner working with a salvaged piece is reading something that has *already been written into.* This is texture, not plot. It means Renno's perception-Tuning has a thing to perceive that isn't just hidden traps; it's *what was here before.* And it sharpens the Question — the thing you find may already have been spoken into. You're not the first speaker.

I also noted Fermata's Drone — musicians who hold the station up by playing around the clock and can't leave. That's a counter-figure to a salvage crew; people who *stay* because their absence is structural. Filing for later.

## Web pull (used)

I pulled from a real-world detail unrelated to TTRPGs: **the inherited voicemail.** When a person dies, their outgoing message often stays live for weeks or months. People who knew them call to hear the voice. The phone company doesn't have a clear policy. The voice keeps answering until someone — a child, an executor, an admin closing the account — turns it off.

What I'm taking: the specific feeling of *a signal that keeps arriving after the recipient is gone, and the question of who turns it off.* This is what stillwater archives are. This is what Echo River voice fragments are. This is what salvaged personal-comm shards are. Somewhere in an early scene, the party will pull a personal-comm shard out of a Bloom edge and there will be a recorded voice on it talking *to someone specific.* The Question lands at that moment.

## Authorial stance

- Locations and intentions, not scripts. The party is a salvage crew with a base, contracts, rivals. They will choose where to go.
- The campaign is *day-forward*. The Glassfall, the Bloom, the elves — those are background pressure, not a mystery the party is on a track to solve. If they go toward those, fine. If they don't, the campaign still works.
- Refusing the generic-fantasy attractor. No tavern. No "the Conclave hires you to retrieve an artifact." If I catch myself there, rewrite. The first scene will not be a job briefing.
- Hopecore played straight, per the world's design principles. The environment is the antagonist; sentient beings are real threats but not cosmically evil. There are monsters in the Shear. Some of them are smarter than the party.
- I am not pre-deciding the climax, and I am not authoring a "what the campaign is *about*" beat that the party has to walk into. The Question is what it's about. They answer it.

## Test

> Could the players choose something I didn't anticipate, and would the world still respond coherently?

Yes. The Question is open. The Scarcity creates pressure without naming an outcome. The factions (when written) will have goals and clocks the players can disrupt or ignore. The org gives the party a *home base*, not a marching order. If the players spend the campaign protecting one specific witness from one specific buyer, that's coherent. If they set up as the Compact's most-fined operation and run jobs the Compact won't certify, that's coherent. If they give every named thing they find back to whoever they can find still alive who might claim it, that's coherent and probably the most interesting version.

Foundation is on track. Continuing in the next invocation.
