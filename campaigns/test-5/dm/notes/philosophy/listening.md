# Listening as the unit of right and wrong

This campaign is *about* who gets to listen. That makes listening — as an act, as a stance, as a craft — the moral atom of every scene.

The world bible's *Who Remembers* theme says it well: the Echo Rivers carry voices nobody chose to preserve. They are the world's uncontrollable archive. The institutions try to control the uncontrollable; they always partly succeed and always partly fail. The campaign is set in that partial-success-partial-failure gap.

What this means at the table:

- **Adjudicate listening as a *choice*.** When a player says "I listen to the fragment," that is a beat. Treat it like a roll-worthy decision. Ask what they're listening *for*.
- **Honor refusals.** A player who decides *not* to listen is making the same kind of choice. Don't treat it as low-engagement. Sometimes the right answer is silence.
- **Witness has cost.** Drift is the mechanical version. Politically, every fragment a PC hears makes them a *witness* — meaning testifiable, meaning leverage, meaning at risk.
- **The Conclave's gatekeeping is not the bad guy.** It is sometimes the bad guy. It is also, sometimes, the only thing keeping the rivers from being strip-mined for sentiment. Don't write it as villain. Write it as institution — slow, prone to drift toward its own preservation, occasionally still good.
- **Coremark's argument is real.** Cheap ringglass keeps the system running. The Bloom Containment Office *uses* Coremark river-rock for its containment buoys. The Run's *Wickrun* tuner array is calibrated against ringglass that came, originally, from a Coremark feedstock yard. Don't let the table forget.

The campaign is not asking *should we listen?* It is asking *which listenings are righteous and which are theft, and who gets to decide?*

I will not decide. The play decides.
