# Drift-Eels

Native to Echo River surfacing pools and the marshy lowlands of the Sable Crescent. Soft-bodied, pale, sightless, mid-band ringglass parasites — they ingest free-floating signal-band crystal dust and excrete it in oriented ribbons, which is part of how the rivers' surfacings stay productive.

## Where encountered

Wherever an Echo River pool sits open to air for more than an hour. Lowstep's edge. Stillsink. The lower channels of the Holdcells. Anywhere a tuned ringglass array runs cold near standing water.

## Behavior

Slow. Curious. Drawn to active resonance — a singing tuner-array, a fragment-shard that's been improperly sealed, a kite-hauler whose engine has been left humming overnight. They do not bite. They *attach* — soft-mouthed suction, harmless to skin, but if attached to a tuned crystal they slowly drift its calibration. A drift-eel on a fragment-shard is a six-hour problem. A drift-eel on a *Wickrun*-grade tuner array is a six-week problem.

## Sense

Pale, wet, faintly humming at 3.1-3.4 kHz — *just* in audible range as a low buzz under the smell of wet rock. They feel rubbery and warm. They smell, faintly, like ringglass dust and old water.

## Mechanical hooks

- HP 4, no damaging attack.
- A crew that doesn't seal a fragment after listening invites drift-eel attachment in any wet location. Treat as a Calibration Drift consequence (ticks a clock; resolved by Tuner specialist, mid-band band, ~4 hours).
- An infestation in a tuner-array is a Major Equipment Consequence — three days minimum out of service, qualified Tuner needed.
- They are, technically, edible. Sable locals do not eat them.

## What they want

To attach. That's it. They are wildlife.
