# Foundation

I want a campaign that sits next to a river of dead voices and asks the table what to do with them. That's the shape.

## The Question

**Who has the right to listen?**

Three questions stacked. *Who*, meaning a person, an institution, an heir, a buyer, a stranger. *Has the right*, meaning by what claim — purchase, blood, profession, presence, none. *To listen*, meaning to take in something said when nobody was meant to be in the room.

I'm not answering any of the three. The play answers them.

The Question pulls because the world has *already* decided who listens. The Echo Ledger Conclave catalogues, redacts, releases. Coremark grinds river-rock to feedstock and treats voices as a side-effect. Private grievers pay a year's wages for a fragment of their daughter's voice. The Tempered Accord prefers nobody discuss this in public. So every scene has a default answer and a working answer, and they will diverge.

## The Scarcity

**Witnesses.**

Not memory — the planet has a literal river of memory under the rock. The scarce thing is *people who heard, who saw, who can speak to what was said.* The Echo Rivers carry voices, but the voices are anonymous, decontextualized, half-erased by the Redaction. The actual witnesses — elven engineers who would have known what they meant, ring-era families, the survivors of the Famine's first decade — are mostly dead, deliberately erased, or hiding. The world keeps trying to fill that gap with institutions that *claim* witness. All those claims are political.

Witnesses points at:
- Why grieving families pay carriers to ferry fragments — they want a witness to their dead.
- Why the Conclave is so guarded — they are the institutional witness, and witness is power.
- Why Coremark's harvesting is genuinely violent — it eats witnesses faster than they can be heard.
- Why the elves' absence is felt *now*, two centuries on — they were the only witnesses who would have understood.

## Authorial stance

Post-Apocalypse-World, post-Wanderhome. Hopecore-with-cost. The world is broken in specific named ways and the people in it are still cooking dinner, fixing kite-cord, naming kids. The Bloom is contained. Sithari runs courts. The buses, broadly, still come. What's wrong is *quieter* than that — it's about what got smoothed out so the buses could keep coming. Stakes register as grief and political access, not extinction events.

Tone targets:
- Specific and slow. One thing per scene. Adjective scarcity.
- Resonance details that show their work — band, bandwidth, cost. If a thing hums, name the frequency.
- Scenes that end one beat before resolution.
- NPCs whose flaws are visible in round one.

## What this is not

- Not "find the elves." If they return, the play finds it.
- Not "stop Coremark." Coremark has a plausible argument and the world has been buying it for sixty years. The party can side with them, fight them, or trade with them.
- Not "uncover the Redaction." The mystery isn't *that* it happened; it's *what specifically* got cut and whose hand was on the edit.
- Not "save the Echo Rivers." The rivers aren't disappearing. They're being *managed*, which is a different problem.

## Texture pulls

**Lore pull (Echo Rivers).** Rivers don't replay on demand; you stand and take what comes. The Conclave calls listener-disorientation "drift." Fragments survive because crystal-saturated water is hard to edit at scale — the Redaction tried and only partly succeeded. *Witnesses survive in the rocks because the eraser couldn't reach the rocks.* Load-bearing fact.

**Web pull (Navajo Nation Library, ~2024).** Five filing cabinets of oral-history reel-to-reel tapes were lost for years; recovered from storage in an empty jail cell. The shape — *witnesses kept in a place built to hold prisoners* — seeds **the Holdcells**, a defunct Sable Crescent enforcement house where pre-Glassfall fragment-shards were stashed two generations ago, and which someone is now quietly trying to reclaim. Detention architecture as accidental archive.

## Connections this is drawing

- The Question and the Scarcity are tied through the rivers. Three factions take three different positions on the tie.
- The party belongs to a small carrier co-op — the **Half-Bar Run** — that ferries Echo River fragments and other resonance-sensitive cargo. They have a reason to be in every scene the campaign needs them in.
- The opening arc is built around a fragment that should have been routine and isn't. The fragment is a witness to something the Redaction missed.

## Done check

- Side with Coremark? Yes.
- Destroy a fragment? Yes.
- Break the Conclave's monopoly? Yes.
- Do nothing and keep running cargo? Yes.

If the answer to all four is yes, the foundation isn't railroading. Foundation holds.
