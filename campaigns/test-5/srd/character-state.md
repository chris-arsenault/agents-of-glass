# Character State

## HP

HP is staying power in dangerous scenes, not a death simulator. It covers
injury, exhaustion, shock, gear strain, and loss of ability to keep acting at
full pace.

At 0 HP, a character is out of the action: captured, unconscious, pinned,
routed, isolated, or otherwise unable to keep acting normally. It does not
automatically mean dead.

## Consequences

A consequence is lasting fictional state that should not drift: cracked ribs,
capture, separation, disgrace, obligation, gear strain, or similar fallout.

Consequences are not a condition engine. They are labels plus prose severity
that the table honors until resolved.

## Inventory

Inventory is arbitrary and narrative. A sword, net, forged seal, climbing rig,
drugged wine, resonance fork, or signed letter can all matter when the fiction
supports it.

Players may cite inventory to justify bonuses, lower risk, larger impact dice,
constraints, changed positioning, or other prose effects. These are examples,
not limits.

Item effect tags are reminders. They are not mechanics by themselves.

## Effects

An effect is named fictional state that changes what future actions can
reasonably do: pinned, bleeding, in cover, exposed, has the room,
signal-jammed.

Most scene effects stay in prose and table state. Lasting PC fallout becomes a
consequence.

## Signature Moves

Each level-1 character starts with one simple signature move: a recurring spell,
maneuver, search style, social tactic, ritual, or other habit. They gain one
additional slot at levels 3, 5, 7, and 9, for five total slots by level 9.

Signature moves are consistency anchors, not guaranteed powers. They give the
table something recognizable and give the DM material the world can notice,
reward, or counter.

Players and the DM can add a move during play when repetition makes it
identity-defining: "I think this is becoming a signature move." Later moves can
be broader or more potent in the fiction, but they still do not become fixed
mechanical buttons.

## Clocks and Trackers

Scene trackers are short-term numeric state for the current scene. Durable
clocks are cross-scene pressure.

At least one action-scene tracker should usually be public so players know what
ending the scene means. Hidden trackers are valid for secret danger or
opposition.
