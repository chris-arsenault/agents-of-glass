# The Line You Hold Is the One You Can See

This campaign is about containment as a specific practice — not as a metaphor. People doing specific things every day to keep something from getting out. The question isn't whether the Bloom can be stopped; Lira's Wall answered that. The question is what happens when the people doing the containing have stopped fully believing that what they're doing is working, and have kept showing up anyway, and have kept their mouths shut, and now the cost of staying quiet is becoming visible.

Obligation without gratitude. Expertise without communication. Trust in instruments rather than people.

The station has been running for eight years. The instruments say it's fine. One person who has been here for seven of those years says it's not fine. The instruments and the one person can't both be right — except that what the instruments are measuring and what the person is noticing might not be the same thing. Instruments are calibrated for what they were built to detect. People notice what falls outside calibration.

The campaign rewards the kind of attention that doesn't require authorization. Looking at the maintenance log. Sitting with Tern Molsk for an hour. Asking the Displaced Quarter's elder what they've seen, because she's been looking at the Zone for eight years from a perspective the Council doesn't have and hasn't asked for.

What the campaign isn't about: heroic intervention. Nobody charges into the Bloom Zone. Nobody fights the institution. The work is information — getting it to the right person in time. The drama is in the gap between what is known and what has been officially recognized.
