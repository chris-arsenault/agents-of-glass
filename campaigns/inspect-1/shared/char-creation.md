# Character Creation — Vestige Point

You are at Vestige Point. The campaign's question is: *What do you owe a thing you've kept alive past the point where someone should have said it out loud?*

The scarcity is trust — specifically, the trust that lets information move between people who need it. Your character is someone for whom that scarcity is personal.

## What you need to establish

Before play begins, know three things about your character:

1. **Why you're at Vestige Point.** It doesn't have to be dramatic. Rotating contract, following someone here, passing through and stayed — any of these is sufficient. What matters is that you're here when Tern Molsk asks someone to look at his data.

2. **Which hook you're tied to.** Pick one of the five below and anchor your character to it. You don't have to be the party's point of entry for that hook. You just need a reason it's not abstract to you.

3. **What you're carrying.** One thing from your past that makes you relevant to the situation — a credential, a relationship, a kind of knowledge, a debt. Not backstory for its own sake. Something that will be useful or complicate things.

## The five hooks

**"Tern asked me to look at something."**
The lead Tuner needs a second set of eyes on anomaly data that's been in the system for three months unread. If you have resonance training of any kind — Clarisant, folk, or even informal exposure — Tern has a specific reason to want you specifically. If you don't, you may still be the one he trusts enough to ask.

**"The supply ship is three days late."**
The Saltmark hasn't docked. Its captain, Deth Orivar, has been at the Karet-Adjacent Waystation and has seen Coremark vessels on the outer arms. If you have a reason to care about the Saltmark — a relationship with the crew, cargo you were expecting, connections to what moves through this corridor — this hook is yours.

**"She was at my wall."**
The Karet Displaced maintain a memorial on their platform. One of the names on that wall is unverifiable through official channels — the Council can't confirm or deny the person existed, because their credential is restricted and expired. If you have a connection to the Karet hab or the Displaced community, you may know whose name that is, or why it matters. You may have your own reasons to want the director's field notebook returned.

**"Someone's been in the Deep Tap."**
The maintenance access at the bottom of the station shows twelve logged entries against one official record. If you do any kind of work that takes you through the station's lower levels — maintenance, inspection, cargo handling, Tuner work on the array housing — you've noticed. Maybe you haven't reported it yet. The reason you haven't is yours to define.

**"The Council is cutting staff."**
The reduction notice is posted in the common area. Tern Molsk is on the list. If you have a reason to care about what happens to the people who've been here for years doing the quiet work — or a reason to care about what happens when they leave — this hook connects you to the campaign's question directly.

## On resonance sensitivity

The world has two kinds of Tuners: Clarisant-trained practitioners who work within institutional bandwidth limits, and folk practitioners who developed sensitivity without formal training and haven't been taught what not to notice.

The monitoring array's primary focus — Ol'Rint, pre-Glassfall ringglass — is detecting something in the narrow bandwidth. Clarisant training doesn't limit what you can sense; it limits what you're trained to report and take seriously. A Clarisant Tuner can interact with Ol'Rint's readings. They will recognize that the signal is real and outside their training parameters.

A folk Tuner — someone who learned from another person, or who came to sensitivity without formal study — may have no particular limitation on bandwidth. They might interface with Ol'Rint in ways a Clarisant practitioner would consider inadvisable. The Clarisant considers that a risk. The folk Tuner may simply experience it as the instrument doing something interesting.

The Knowledge-Decay Loop is real here: the elves operated routinely at narrow bandwidth. The Tuners who came after rebuilt from fragments. Some of what got rebuilt is better adapted to current conditions than recovered theory would be. If you're a folk Tuner, this is the relevant context.

## Species notes

- **Gnomes** have a crystal-laced nervous system that picks up Bloom Zone resonance directly. Being near the cordon boundary isn't neutral for a gnome. Chel'sten is a gnome, and this is part of what informs their calculation about acceptable risk.
- **Sithari** in this region often carry two-part names (personal + lineage). Senna Kovrath is Sithari.
- **Orcs** in this region typically use a single given name. Duva is an orc.
- **Hab-worlders** — humans whose families have lived in ring habitats for multiple generations — often have clipped, functional names. Tern Molsk is a hab-worlder.

## What you don't need to establish

You don't need a reason to care about Bloom Zone containment in the abstract. You need a reason to care about *what is specifically happening here*, between *these specific people*. The campaign's question is about obligation and information and trust — your character should be someone for whom those things have texture.

You don't need a backstory. You need a present-tense hook and something to carry.
