# Foundation — Working framing

This is mine. Not the players'. I'll keep a tighter version at `context.md`
for the table.

## The Question

**Who answers the call when the system that was supposed to answer it can't anymore?**

Not "should the system be replaced." Not "is the system worth saving." The
Question sits one rung lower than that: a call has gone out, the official
channel has not picked up, and somebody is going to either step in or not. I
want every major scene to surface a version of that — a containment the
Council can't reach, a salvage Vantara has dropped from its routes, a Tuner
who needs a hand and the Clarisant office is six weeks away. Then the
players decide.

The Question is doing three things for me:

- It refuses the rescue-fantasy gradient. The system isn't a villain. It's
  *thin*. Stretched, defunded, distracted, slow. The faction map will
  reflect that — no Big Evil, lots of well-intentioned bodies that can't
  cover the ground they used to cover.
- It opens up a real range of answers. Step in. Refuse. Try and fail. Build
  something parallel. Force the official channel to actually show up.
  Become the channel and discover the costs of being it. All of those are
  legitimate and all of them are scenes.
- It puts the players in a position where doing nothing has weight. The
  call doesn't get answered for free. Someone notices.

## The Scarcity

**Answers.**

Echo Rivers full of voices nobody chose to preserve and nobody can reply to.
Synod rituals that work and no one can explain. Bloom Zones that are
contained but not understood. Duskgrain that holds power and there's nobody
who can release it cleanly. The elves left, and the question of *what was
this for* now hangs over every piece of inherited infrastructure.

Trust, time, signal — all already spoken for in the lore. *Answers* is the
one that isn't, and it cuts across all of them. It points the NPCs at what
they want (someone who actually knows). It points the conflicts at what
they're really about (whose guess gets to be canon). It gives the players a
posture: a party that takes calls is a party that has to decide, often,
whether they have an answer worth giving.

## What I'm pulling on

**From the loops:** Knowledge Decay (system → overuse → loss → reinvention)
is the engine under everything. The Reconnection thread tells me the
emotional given: communication is sacred. The combination is the sweet
spot — communication is sacred *and* the people who built the systems we
communicate through are gone, so every channel is improvised. Every channel
fails differently.

**From themes:** *Who decides what gets remembered* and *what do you build
when the original blueprints are gone.* I'm not running both of those at
full intensity — too much. The campaign is mostly Builders Gone, with
Who Remembers showing up as a recurring B-side whenever the Echo Rivers,
the Conclave, or salvaged ring-era kit walk into a scene.

**From the world bible's specific texture:**

- *Kite-cord prices doubled after the Conclave seizure* (ringglass entry).
  Mundane economic shockwaves that the players will feel without anyone
  having to lecture about them.
- *The Span can't be sabotaged — intent-responsive.* Some inherited tech
  knows what you're trying to do and refuses. That's a beat I want
  available.
- *Drift* — the Conclave's word for the dream-state listeners fall into.
  I'm going to lean on that word. It's usable for more than the rivers.
- *Duskgrain releases across the full spectrum simultaneously.* If a piece
  of duskgrain ever shows up in a scene it is going to *matter*.

## Web pull

I read about a wave of small-town volunteer fire departments closing or
losing contracts across 2026 — Norwich CT shut down a 137-year-old
volunteer company over a contract dispute, Franklin and Bannertown in NC
won't see their contracts renewed past June, an entire department in
Tennessee resigned at once. The texture I'm pulling is not "underdog
firefighters." It's more specific: *the fire siren still tests itself at
noon. The engine in the bay is still polished. Nobody's coming when the
call goes out, and everyone in town has had to stop pretending otherwise.*
That's the emotional shape I want for the Shear-edge places the campaign
opens around. The official infrastructure is still *there*. It just
doesn't run on real calls anymore. The party's organization grew up in
that gap.

## Authorial stance

I am not writing a story about heroes who fix the system. I am writing a
working table where, week to week, calls come in, players answer some and
not others, and the world keeps moving whether they show up or not. The
Continuity is not the antagonist. The Council is not the antagonist. The
Bloom is not the antagonist. The antagonist, if there is one, is the
gradient that says *somebody else will handle it* — and the long, patient
list of consequences when nobody does.

I want hopecore in the Sly-Flourish sense: the loss is real, the cost is
real, what grows from it is genuinely new. I am not interested in despair
posture. I am also not interested in restoration fantasy. Adaptation is
what the loop produces; that's where the meaning lives.

## What I am explicitly *not* doing

- A grand-conspiracy unmasking arc. The Question doesn't survive one of
  those.
- A "find the lost elven knowledge" arc. The whole point of the loop is
  that recovery isn't the answer; reinvention is.
- An overarching prophecy. No.
- The party as the only people who can possibly do this. There are other
  crews. The Compact has its own people. The Council is doing its job
  badly because it's understaffed, not because it's incompetent. The
  players are *one* set of hands.
