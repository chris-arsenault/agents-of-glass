# Builders Gone, Maintainers Still Climbing

The campaign's primary thematic territory.

The elves built the resonance infrastructure and left. What's left
behind isn't ruins — it's a *working system without its makers*. The
ringglass still hums. The Span still routes. The kettle plate
heats. The Hollow still transmits weekly. None of it was ever
explained.

The campaign sits one rung below the question *can we recover the
old knowledge.* It assumes recovery is not the answer. The honest
question is: **how do you maintain something whose blueprints you
do not have, when the maintenance ritual is the only thing left
holding it up?**

I read about a lighthouse keeper this week — the last one in the
United States Coast Guard, who retired in 2023 after twenty years
on Boston Light. The light itself was automated in 1998. She kept
climbing the tower twice a day for the next twenty-five years.
The light didn't need her. The keeping is what was left after the
need.

That texture is the campaign. The party's home is a station whose
kettle plate has been on for fourteen years on a kinetic-tuned
ringglass salvage that nobody fully understands. Its antenna damper
is being maintained by two different hands tuning it in two
different directions, and one of those hands has not been
identified. Its founder left four years ago and the kit she
salvaged is what the cooperative still runs on. The Council's
Halsworn cordon is at quarter-coverage and Veni Karst is on
rotation alone, replacing buoys whose damping fluid is the
Conclave's history. Sable's silent ledger is nine years deep
because someone needed to be kept fueled. Threshold-Rim has not
answered comms in eight years and Dern's daughter is on it.

Everyone is climbing the tower. The light doesn't always know.

The campaign's job is not to ask whether the climbing is *worth
it* — that is a question that destroys play, because the answer
arrives too cleanly. The campaign's job is to ask **what does the
climber owe to the next climber.** When the keeper retires, what
gets handed to whoever shows up. When Vez left, what did she
hand Dern. When Dern stops, what does she hand the rest. When
the Council formally closes Halsworn, who is the cordon's next
keeper. When the kettle plate eventually fails — and it will, the
microcavities under it are not an indefinite resource — what was
the kettle *for*, in the end.

That is the territory. Inheritance without instructions.
Adaptation, not restoration. The hopecore version of *Builders
Gone* — what you build when the blueprints are gone is genuinely
new, and the people who build it are the people who happen to
be there.

## What this rules out

- **Restoration arcs.** No "recover the elven knowledge" plot. The
  whole loop is that recovery isn't the answer.
- **Conspiracy unmaskings.** The makers did not leave a secret to
  unmask. They left a system. The system runs.
- **Ancient evil.** No.
- **Chosen ones.** The Dropline is one set of hands. There are
  other crews. The Compact has its own people. The Council is
  doing its job badly because it is understaffed, not because it
  is incompetent.

## What this rules in

- **Quiet maintenance scenes that matter more than they should.**
  Rusk closing the recognition folder. Dern walking the workshop
  before a vote. Stillness retuning Bonecord. The kettle being on.
- **Choices about what to keep climbing for.** The Halsworn cordon
  versus the recognition review versus the Stillrun audit versus
  the body in the drift. The Dropline cannot answer all of them.
- **Inheritance moments.** When someone hands something to someone
  else and both of them know it is the inheritance. Mara reads
  these as the campaign's emotional spine.

## How I want to adjudicate scenes against this

- When a player proposes recovery as a solution, ask what would be
  *adapted* from it instead. Recovery is allowed. Restoration is
  not the answer.
- When a system is failing, the world is not asking *can it be
  saved.* It is asking *who will keep climbing it, and at what
  cost.*
- When someone hands something off, slow down. That is the
  campaign's emotional spine. Do not rush the handoff scene.
