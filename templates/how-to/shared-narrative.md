# Shared Narrative Examples

Glass Frontier plays closer to Fate-style shared authorship than a DM-only
description loop. Use details to act, not to stall.

## Better Than Asking

Instead of:

> Is there anything on the dock I can use to jam the door?

Try:

> I kick a loose loading chock under the door and lean my shoulder into it.

Instead of:

> What do I find in the ledger?

Try:

> I turn to the refusal entries and find row 17 has my initials in the clerk
> column.

Instead of:

> Does the duke have any visible weakness?

Try:

> I notice the duke keeps touching the unopened petition stack whenever the
> room mentions public testimony.

## Keep It Load-Bearing

Good invented details point the scene toward a decision:

- the loose chock makes the door-hold into a concrete action
- the ledger row turns vague suspicion into a specific question
- the duke's gesture gives the party leverage to test

Weak invented details expand the scene without resolving anything:

- a new mysterious stranger appears with a second problem
- the ledger contains five unrelated clues
- the duke's whole secret plan is revealed with no cost or roll

## Leave Room For Consequence

When your detail would settle something important, attach risk to it:

> I find my initials on row 17. If that means I signed it, I roll to keep my
> face still before anyone else sees.

Or leave a hook for the DM:

> I find the row, but not the reason code. The missing code is the thing I need
> before I can decide whether to speak.

The goal is not to win authorship. The goal is to give the table a concrete
next step.
