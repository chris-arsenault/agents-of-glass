# Party Organization

The party organization is the first high-fidelity signal. It should constrain
character creation before a campaign mystery, pressure board, or opening arc
exists.

## Action-First Baseline

A good party organization gives players permission to make characters who do
things on screen. Its normal work should put the crew in dangerous places with
people to protect, hostile forces to outmaneuver, physical obstacles to cross,
and weird problems that cannot be solved by permission alone.

The organization can have law, records, hierarchy, rituals, and procedure, but
those are support structures. They should not be the main activity. If the most
natural scene for the organization is a meeting, hearing, audit, desk review,
chain-of-custody argument, or claims dispute, redesign the organization until
its natural scenes are rescue, breach, chase, containment, field repair,
infiltration, expedition, evacuation, confrontation, or dangerous negotiation in
a place where something can go wrong physically.

## What A Good Org Brief Does

- Tells players what kind of working unit this is.
- Names the dangerous situations the unit physically enters.
- Implies what jobs, tools, leverage, habits, and overlaps the group needs.
- Shows who can be hurt if the unit fails or delays.
- Gives enough public structure that players can fit characters into it without
  optimizing around unseen campaign prep.

## In-World Shape Test

The organization should feel native to the current campaign world while still
being broad enough for different kinds of PCs. Tie it to existing public lore,
but do not make the lore entry do all the work. The organization needs its own
distinct appetite, internal culture, outside pressure, action-facing work, and
reason for people with different methods to stay together.

## Ensemble Shape Check

Use ensemble adventure fiction only as a cast-function check, not as the borrowed
detail source and not as a list of character templates.

Ask what the unit needs:

- Who gets people out when the place starts killing them?
- Who opens, blocks, breaks, repairs, or rigs the physical path?
- Who spots the hazard before it lands?
- Who talks under threat while the room is still moving?
- Who carries trouble, misdirection, or the bold bad idea?
- Who keeps tools, supplies, transport, or extraction working under pressure?
- Who records, authorizes, or explains the action after the crew survives it?
- Which role overlaps with another when the team is small?

## Non-Adjacent Pull

Use a current nonfiction source about real people or groups under pressure. Do
not seed the search with team categories, job titles, emergency-response terms,
fiction, RPG advice, or campaign advice. The point is to import human texture
first, then translate it into an action-facing organization.

Borrow concrete observed behavior, not vibes. The pull should change what the
organization wants when no mission is active; how members show status, trust,
rivalry, or belonging; what objects matter; how conflict appears; what rituals
or habits shape the group; what scenes the organization naturally creates; and
what physical situations the group sends characters into.

Avoid pulls whose strongest details are only paperwork, accreditation,
liability, recordkeeping, or chain of custody. Those can sharpen texture, but
they should never become the primary fantasy of the organization.

## Private Note Rule

Before character creation, keep `dm/notes/organization.md` broad. Write
compromises, secrets, and tensions that belong to the org. Do not write named
player forecasts or "this is Tev's lane" style prep yet.
