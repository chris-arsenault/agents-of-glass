# Archetypes And Tone

Archetype is the broad class-like identity people would use after the character
becomes a story. Choose the name that would still describe them at level 20,
when they are a mythic figure in the campaign world. It can be practical,
mystical, martial, musical, or strange, but it must feel playable and legendary,
not like a job title.

Do not use a current workplace function as the archetype. Recorder, clerk,
examiner, witness, handler, liaison, archivist, and coordinator are jobs or
organization roles unless they are sharpened into an action-mythic identity.
Ask: what would a tavern song, wanted poster, field manual, or frightened enemy
call this person after the campaign has made them famous?

The tone constraint is not "avoid funny class names." A strange or dryly funny
archetype is fine if the character and world take it seriously. The target is
hope-core with wit, pressure, and sincerity: people may be funny, but the world
is not a parody of itself.

Organization role answers a different question: how does this person belong to
the party's organization?

Useful organization roles:

- founder of the independent crew
- long-time member with high trust
- probationary hire
- cook and morale center
- quartermaster
- outsider specialist on retainer
- family legacy member
- debt-bound recruit
- official porter, unofficial fixer
- route contact
- junior member everyone underestimates
- senior member everyone avoids disappointing
- public face of the crew
- person who knows where everything is
- new hire with one powerful sponsor
- old friend of the founder
- the one nobody remembers hiring

Tone examples:

- A Legendary Trombonist can be funny at the table because carrying a battered
  horn into a Bloom cordon is funny. Play it seriously anyway: the horn has a
  lineage, the technique has costs, and somebody in the world knows exactly why
  the third slide is wrapped in black thread.
- A Funk Paladin can have a ridiculous nickname and still be an oath-bound
  defender whose rhythm discipline holds a frightened crowd together.
- A Jazz Wizard can be dry, improvisational, and impossible to schedule. The
  magic is still real, the craft is still learned, and the consequences still
  land.

The dry humor belongs in how people live under pressure. Do not turn the
setting into a bit.
